  3. The actual files (not summaries or descriptions).
  4. PDF format if available, and if it's an image-based PDF (like a scan), you want OCR (Optical Character Recognition) applied to make the text searchable.

Court cases, especially complex commercial litigation like this one, can have hundreds of exhibits. Finding them and getting the unredacted versions presents several hurdles:

  • PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records): This is the primary system for accessing federal court documents in the United States. You'll need a PACER account (which has fees). The case you mentioned, Card Connect, LLC v. Shift4 Payments, LLC, is likely a federal case, so PACER is the starting point. You'll need the case number and the court where it was filed. A quick search reveals this is likely case number 2:20-cv-02432-JDW in the Eastern District of Pennsylvania.

  • Docket Sheet: Once you find the case on PACER, the first thing to look at is the docket sheet. This is a chronological list of every filing in the case. Exhibits are often attached to motions, briefs, declarations, and other filings. The docket sheet will tell you which filings have attachments.

  • Identifying Relevant Filings: You'll need to carefully read the docket entries to identify filings that are likely to contain exhibits related to text messages and risk. Look for keywords like:

    • "Motion for Summary Judgment" (these often have extensive exhibits)
    • "Declaration of [Person's Name]" (declarations often introduce evidence)
    • "Brief in Support of..." or "Brief in Opposition to..."
    • "Expert Report"
    • "Deposition Transcript" (exhibits might be marked during depositions)
    • Any filing mentioning "communications," "text messages," "emails," "risk assessment," "risk management," "fraud," "breach of contract," etc.

  • Redaction Problem: This is the biggest hurdle. Court documents, especially exhibits containing private communications or sensitive business information, are almost always redacted to protect:

    • Personally Identifiable Information (PII): Names, addresses, phone numbers, Social Security numbers, etc.
    • Confidential Business Information: Trade secrets, financial data, internal strategies.
    • Privacy: Information that would be an unwarranted invasion of personal privacy if disclosed.
    • Privileged Information: Attorney-client communications, etc.

    Getting unredacted exhibits is extremely difficult, often requiring a court order. You would typically need to demonstrate a compelling legal reason why you need the unredacted information, and that need would have to outweigh the privacy and confidentiality interests of the parties involved. Simply being curious or wanting the information for research is not sufficient.

  • Sealed Documents: This one is impossible to get without going through proper channels in court.
