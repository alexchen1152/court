Key Observations and Analysis, drawing inferences from the exhibits:

  • Zero-Click Development and Concerns: The text messages reveal that Shift4 was actively developing the "Zero-Click Vector." There were initial plans for it, discussions about testing, and even a projected production date. However, concerns about security were raised by Jared Isaacman (Shift4's CEO).
  • Shift in Plans: The messages show a clear shift. Initially, the Zero-Click vector was intended to replace the existing vector process. Later, Shift4 communicated that Zero-Click would not be used, and the Fiserv employee did confirm this final answer.
  • Technical Details: The messages confirm that the Zero-Click vector involved a different endpoint for tokenization requests. This is crucial because, as the diagrams in Exhibit 23 illustrate, this change bypassed security controls like iFrame source domain validation and CORS.
  • Vulnerability: The combination of the text messages and the diagram strongly suggests that Shift4 was aware of the security implications of the Zero-Click vector. They knew it would be handled differently or avoid some securities.
  • Intent: While the messages don't explicitly state malicious intent, the sequence of events—developing a method to bypass security, discussing its implementation, and then ultimately deciding (ostensibly) not to use that specific named method—raises questions about whether Shift4 used the knowledge gained during Zero-Click development to achieve the same circumvention through other means. This is a central point of contention in the lawsuit.
