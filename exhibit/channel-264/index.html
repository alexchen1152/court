4. The Problem of Redactions

This is the most significant hurdle. Court documents, especially exhibits containing sensitive information like personal conversations, financial data, or trade secrets, are frequently redacted. Redactions are blacked-out portions of the text intended to protect privacy or confidentiality.

  • Unredacted documents are rarely publicly available. Even on PACER, you will almost always encounter redacted versions of exhibits unless you are a party to the case with special access granted by the court.
  • Obtaining unredacted versions usually requires a court order. You would typically need to demonstrate a compelling legal reason why you need access to the unredacted information, and the court would weigh that against the privacy and confidentiality interests of the parties involved.

5. OCR and PDF Conversion

Once an exhibit is obtained (in a redacted form, most likely), dealing with the format is a separate issue:

  • PACER provides PDFs: Most documents on PACER are already in PDF format.
  • Scanned Images: If an exhibit is a scan of a physical document (like printed text messages), it might be an image-based PDF, meaning the text isn't selectable.
  • OCR Software: You would need to use OCR software to convert the image to searchable text. Many tools can do this, including:
    • Adobe Acrobat Pro (paid)
    • Various online OCR services (some free, some paid)
    • Open-source OCR engines like Tesseract
