Key Observations and Context from these Exhibits:

  • Internal Control Weaknesses: The testimony of J. Robert Brown confirms that CardConnect had "material weaknesses in internal controls" that were discovered during the First Data due diligence process. This was considered an "unpleasant surprise" and raised concerns about the potential for misstated revenue.
  • Text Message Tone: The text messages between J.D. Oder, II and Robert Carr reveal a very specific, and arguably manipulative, strategy surrounding the transition of CardConnect's systems to Shift4. They express skepticism about the motives of the former CardConnect team, referring to their offers of "help" with suspicion ("I just think it's funny that they are pretending to help.").
  • "Over Complicating Things": is said by Carr that former CardConnect employee believes the conversion from Lighthouse to Bridge is being over thought.
  • Coding and Control: There's a clear resistance to allowing the former CardConnect team to write code for the new system ("I'm a hard no on them writing the code."). They discuss the possibility of the former team building in "revenue shares" and manipulating the numbers. The concern is that the old team could set them up to fail.
  • Risk of Data Loss: Oder mentions a key risk: "If they code a new system, we lose all historical data." This highlights the importance of data preservation and the potential consequences of a poorly planned transition.
  • Deception and Protection: Oder says, "I hate being deceptive, but sometimes you have to protect yourself." This suggests an awareness that their actions may not be entirely transparent. Carr will act as a messenger of anything discussed.
  • Strategic Delay: Carr states, "We wouldn't want them to get it done too early." This, combined with the discussion of using the former team's project plan to inform their own planning with Bridge, suggests a deliberate attempt to control the timing of the transition and possibly delay the former CardConnect team's efforts.
  • "Nashville" System Carr and Oder discusses that the prior ins and outs of the "Nashville System" has built in revenue shares.
