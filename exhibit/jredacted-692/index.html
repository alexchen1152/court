Finding the Case and Exhibits

To locate exhibits from a court case, these are the typical sources:

  • PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records): This is the primary federal court records system in the United States. It requires a paid account. You can search by case name or number. Exhibits are often (but not always) filed as attachments to docket entries (documents filed in the case).
  • Court Website: Some courts (especially at the state level) have their own online record systems, which may be free or have different fee structures than PACER.
  • Court Clerk's Office: You can often request copies of documents (including exhibits) directly from the court clerk's office, either in person or by mail/email. There are usually fees for copies.
  • Third-Party Legal Research Services: Services like Westlaw, LexisNexis, and Bloomberg Law have extensive court records, including exhibits. These are subscription-based services.
  • RECAP: Recap is Pacer spelled backwards. it a free archive of Pacer filings.

Challenges and Important Considerations

  • Availability: Not all exhibits are digitized and available online. Older cases, or cases from smaller courts, may have limited online records. Physical copies might be the only option.
  • Sealed Records: Some exhibits might be filed under seal, meaning they are not publicly accessible due to confidentiality concerns (trade secrets, personal information, etc.). You would need a court order to unseal them.
  • Exhibit Format: Exhibits can be in any format: documents, photos, videos, audio recordings, physical objects. Finding the specific ones you want (text messages and EXIF GPS data) requires careful review of the exhibit list and the documents themselves.
  • Redactions: Even if you request unredacted documents, the court may have already redacted them before making them public. Overturning redactions usually requires a legal challenge.
  • Cost: Pacer and subscription services needs a paid account.
