Why Unredacted Exhibits are Very Difficult to Obtain

Court records, including exhibits, are generally public. However, there are strong protections for sensitive information. Here's why getting completely unredacted versions is extremely difficult, and often impossible, without being a party to the case and having a court order:

  • Privacy Concerns: Text messages and /Library/Biome data inherently contain Personally Identifiable Information (PII). This includes names, phone numbers, addresses, potentially health data, financial information, and details about individuals' movements and habits. Courts are legally obligated to protect this information under various privacy laws (e.g., GDPR, CCPA, and others).
  • Trade Secrets: In a case involving technology companies, exhibits often contain proprietary information, source code, business strategies, customer lists, and other trade secrets. Companies go to great lengths to protect this information, and courts routinely seal or redact such content.
  • Protective Orders: During litigation, parties almost always enter into protective orders. These are agreements, approved by the court, that govern how sensitive information is handled. They specify what can be designated as "Confidential" or "Highly Confidential - Attorneys' Eyes Only." These designations determine who can see the information and how it can be used.
  • Sealing Orders: In some cases, entire exhibits or portions of the record are sealed, meaning they are not available to the public at all. This happens when the need for confidentiality outweighs the public's right to access.
