2. Docket Entry 65-19, Exhibit 18: VirusTotal Reports

This exhibit contains several VirusTotal reports. I'll provide the key information from a few representative examples. Key data points typically include:

  • File Name: The name of the file analyzed.
  • SHA256 Hash: A unique cryptographic fingerprint of the file.
  • Detection Ratio: The number of antivirus engines that detected the file as malicious / the total number of engines that scanned it.
  • First Submission / Last Analysis Date: Dates indicating when the file was first uploaded to VirusTotal and when it was last analyzed.
  • File Type: e.g., "Win32 EXE" (Windows executable), "PDF", etc.
  • Community Score: A score assigned by the VirusTotal community reflecting their assessment of the file.
  • Tags: Descriptive words, often AI generated

II. SHIFT4’S BREACHES AND FAILURES

Shift4 has violated numerous covenants and obligations in the Transaction Documents, including without limitation the following:

A. Failure to Engage in Good Faith Efforts

Both Transaction Documents obligate the Parties to act in good faith. Shift4 has consistently failed to meet these requirements to date.

Section 7.9(a) of the Purchase Agreement obligates Buyer to, and to cause its Affiliates to, “use commercially reasonable, good faith efforts to . . . obtain approvals of all Governmental Authorities and Payment Networks necessary, proper or advisable to effect the transactions [effectuated thereby].” This creates an unambiguous obligation of good faith on the part of Shift4 to close on the Contingency Operations no later than June 28, 2019 (the “Outside Date”).

This obligation is further reinforced by Section 7.2(a) of the Purchase Agreement, requiring the Parties to “cooperate fully, and . . . use all commercially reasonable efforts as would be used by a prudent and sophisticated party acting in good faith in their dealings with third parties to take, or cause to be taken, all actions, and to do, or cause to be done, and to assist and cooperate with the other Parties in doing, all things reasonably necessary, proper or advisable under applicable Laws, Payment Network Rules, or otherwise to consummate and make effective the transactions [contemplated by the Purchase Agreement].” Again, this language, agreed to by Shift4, imposes a duty of good faith on Shift4, both to close on the Contingency Operations by the Outside Date, and to assist and cooperate with Seller in good faith prior thereto.

Likewise, Sections 5 and 6 of the Processing Agreement dictate that the Parties act in good faith with respect to specific requirements thereof, and Section 32(a) requires that, generally, “[e]ach party’s exercise of its discretion, rights, and remedies under [the Processing Agreement] shall at all times be subject to such party’s implied duty of good faith and fair dealing under applicable law.” These provisions mirror those cited in the Purchase Agreement and, accordingly, imply an overarching requirement of good faith efforts and fair dealing.

Shift4 has consistently failed to meet any of these obligations to date. For example, Shift4 has:

Failed to implement pricing for new customers in a manner that is consistent with the Processing Agreement. Shift4 has repeatedly tried to re-trade pricing terms, implement arbitrary fees, and manipulate other contractual terms or pricing mechanics to avoid the clear terms of the contract. This has caused significant losses, damage and merchant attrition to Seller.

Failed to timely convert Merchants, forcing Seller to pay significant, unanticipated sums to third party processors.

Failed to provide reasonably equivalent processing services to Seller’s Merchants, as required by Sections 2 and 5 of the Processing Agreement.

Failed to provide reasonable pricing, terms and service, resulting in significant merchant attrition.

Failed to deal in good faith with Seller regarding boarding Merchants, in violation of Section 5(b) of the Processing Agreement, refusing to accommodate reasonable or critical deadlines and to take reasonable actions.

Repeatedly demanded to re-trade agreement terms and threatened to breach the contract, including the Contingency Operations closing, if Seller did not acquiesce to its unreasonable demands. This conduct violates a variety of express and implied obligations in the Transaction Documents.

Failed to obtain approvals of all Governmental Authorities and Payment Networks necessary, proper or advisable to effect the transactions contemplated by the Purchase Agreement, in violation of Section 7.9(a) thereof,

Refused to engage in good faith discussions with Seller in order to meet the deadlines for the Contingency Operations closing, including waiting months to provide a draft of the Contingency Operations Agreement and subsequently failing to respond to Seller’s comments thereto in a timely manner.

The Letter continues for several more pages outlining further alleged breaches, all concerning failing to meet contractual obligations. The letter then concludes.
