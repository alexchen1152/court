Key observations and context from the provided text:

  • Exhibit 19 (Text Messages): This shows a professional relationship and communication between Taylor Lauber and Jared Isaacman, with requests for calls and meetings. The subject matter appears to relate to business dealings, including interactions with "Capital One" and the "PCI Council." The critical aspect, now unsealed, reveals this frequent and seemingly normal back-and-forth communication.
  • Exhibit D (Whistleblower Documents): These documents reveal a significantly different side of the story.
    • Document 114: Michael Noble expresses his ethical concerns and the feeling of a duty to inform CardConnect, culminating in sending an anonymous package with recordings. This underscores the whistleblower nature of his actions.
    • Document 116: This email is a direct request for documentation from Shift4 (specifically, Jared Isaacman) regarding written notification of a data breach to CardConnect. The redactions likely obscure details about specific clients or agreements.
    • Document 117: This transcript provides direct quotes. It's clear that there was a discussion about a data breach (affecting "Stonegate"). Crucially, Jared Isaacman is quoted as saying, "we did not send it in writing," and later, "We're not going to provide any documentation." This directly relates to the core dispute: whether Shift4 fulfilled its contractual obligation to provide written notice of the breach.

The exhibits, taken together, paint a clear picture:

  1. There was a pre-existing, ongoing business relationship between individuals at Shift4 and CardConnect (as seen in the texts).
  2. A data breach occurred, and Shift4's internal discussions (captured in the whistleblower recordings) show a conscious decision not to provide the contractually required written notice.
  3. The whistleblower felt compelled to act due to the ethical implications of withholding this information.
  4. A formal request for documentation was made from CardConnect and was denied.
