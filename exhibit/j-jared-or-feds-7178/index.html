Case Background

This case, filed in the US District Court for the Eastern District of Pennsylvania (Case No. 2:19-cv-01304-TJS), involves a contract dispute between Card Connect, LLC (a subsidiary of Fiserv) and Shift4 Payments, LLC. Card Connect alleged that Shift4 breached a Reseller Agreement by improperly soliciting Card Connect's merchants and failing to pay certain fees. Shift4 counterclaimed, alleging that Card Connect engaged in anticompetitive behavior and breached the agreement themselves.

    I write in response to your letter, dated June 5, 2017, inquiring about the Department of Justice's efforts to "protect consumers and businesses, especially those operating in the financial services sector, from illegitimate and unlawful enterprises." I share your interest in ensuring the Department's resources are trained on pursuing legitimate claims and putting an end to enforcements based on vague and subjective standards.

    In 2013, certain Justice Department officials, in conjunction with various other federal agencies, undertook the law enforcement initiative known as "Operation Choke Point." This initiative was premised on the belief that as financia1 institutions provide the "oxygen" that enables fraudulent merchants to engage in unlawful conduct, the Department could more effectively thwart wrongdoing by focusing on those institutions. After consulting with Department leadership, I directed several policy changes consistent with this view beginning in 2013.

    As part of Operation Choke Point, the Department issued subpoenas to banks for records relating to suspicious merchants. In some instances, the Department pursued civil and criminal investigations, resulting in monetary settlements and criminal charges. In 2014, the House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform investigated Operation Choke Point and found that the Department's "ill-considered" and "inappropriate" initiative forced financial institutions to terminate relationships "with a wide variety of entirely legitimate and lawful businesses," and that the Department had "forfeited" its "responsibility to carefully separate legitimate merchants from bad actors."

    We share your view that law abiding businesses should not be targeted simply for operating in an industry that is disfavored by the current administration. As such, all of the Department's investigations conducted as part of Operation Choke Point are now over, the initiative is no longer in effect, and it will not be undertaken again.

    Thank you again for your letter. Please do not hesitate to contact this office again if we may be of assistance on this or any other matter.

    Sincerely,

    Stephen E. Boyd Assistant Attorney General

Summary and Analysis in context of your request

  • "J" and "Jared": Jared Isaacman, founder and CEO of Shift4, is frequently mentioned and is the author/recipient of many emails. "J" is often used as a shortened form of address or reference to Jared.
  • "Feds": The most direct reference to "feds" is in the July 13, 2017 email chain discussing the end of "Operation Choke Point."   • Context of the Emails: The emails cover a range of topics:
    • Business Strategy: Discussions about acquiring large ("elephant") merchants, growing Shift4's processing business, and internal goals (e.g., the 100% merchant processing goal).
    • Internal Disagreements: Debates about how to handle referrals and the best long-term strategy for Shift4.
    • Operation Chock Point: The one email that confirms the end of this, which affected how credit card processors did business with certain industries.
    • Project Titan/Enterprise-Level Deals: Multiple discussions about strategic, very large deals

The emails cited by each side are used to prove different, and conflicting, claims. Shift4 uses the Operation Choke Point email to say that it was simply passing along relevant and important industry information, and uses the quotes regarding "elephant deals" to emphasize those merchants were outside of the disputed contract. CardConnect/Fiserv use the John Yoder/Jared Isaacman exchanges to show that Shift4 began to aggressively purse its own processing clients, in what it claims is violation of their agreement.
